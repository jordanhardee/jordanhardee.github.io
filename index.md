## Who, What, When, Where

Hello, my name is Jordan Hardee. I am 23 years old and I am running for City Council in Mason, Ohio for the 2019 general election. I am running on a Green platform
with the endorsement of the [Ohio Green Party](https://ohiogp.org).

## Why
Mason City Council is extremely conservative, and I think there are enough progressives in Mason to entail us to at least one seat on city council. But of course the answer to the question of why I'm running is much deeper than that.

If you weren't aware, Mason City Council can be a bit of a circus. There is so much in-fighting, so much toxicity, so much partisan non-sense. The people of Mason deserve better.
I really believe if elected that I can help bring City Council together and help to make it the efficient organ of civil power it was supposed to be. There comes a point in time when you realize "if I don't do this, I'm not sure anyone else will". Watching a City Council meeting on IRCTV as I often do, I had this
realization. "Someone has to do something", so I decided to run for office. 

I get it, I'm young. Relative to most people in government, I am very young. Heck, like most people my age, especially with the COVID pandemic, I live with my parents and we don't even agree on politics. But I don't see this as a weakness, rather I see it as an asset. America is a gerentocracy, what it
needs more than anything else is young blood in the reams of government. If you don't even know how the modern world works you shouldn't be writing laws for it. We're long overdue for an overhaul. The world belongs to the youth.

I have been an activist and an avid student of political theory for many years now. I have studied political thinkers from Aristotle, to Hobbes, to Habermas. If I had to say
what thinkers most inspired me, the answer would change depending on the time. If I had to say where I stand politically today, I would say in ethos I am an advocate of the ideals
of the American thinker Murray Bookchin, in logos I am a cautious Jeffersonian. In the tradition of Murray Bookchin I'd consider myself an advocate of Social Ecology, 
libertarian municipalism, and Communalism. But it's hard to say "oh this word perfectly describes me". Perhaps these words have no meaning to you. Let me be blunt then in expressing my views as they pertain to the local level:

>“The assumption that what currently exists must necessarily exist is the acid that corrodes all visionary thinking.”
> ― Murray Bookchin 

What is to be done? We have to think globally, and act locally.

Climate change is a serious issue and if we aren't addressing it at the local level we should be ashamed of ourselves. We have to
protect local biodiversity. Every bit of land that can be turned into cheap real estate should not be torn down and farmed for tax revenue. We have to rethink landscaping in order to promote native
biodiversity, this would both save costs and make public lands more aesthetically pleasing. I was a big supporter of #SaveTheMasonWetlands. The ecological crisis is a social crisis. 
The domination of man by man is a prerequisite to the domination of nature by man. It is exceptionally difficult to address the climate crisis globally without
simultaneously addressing a social system that allows one man to profit off of the destruction of an entire ecosystem or community. That's privitizing profits while socializing the costs. 
As effective as a Pigovian tax would be in limiting the self-destructive behaviour of the market, real solutions require more radical action, and that can only be a matter for the people themselves. I am in favor of incentivizing
democratic alternatives to traditional capitalist enterprises at the local level, promoting ecologically sustainable farming, and green energy. 

We call ourselves a "community" and don't even know our nextdoor neighbor's names. We need to actively encourage (after COVID) more direct, participatory forms of democracy 
(town halls, neighborhood assemblies, public forums) and community engagement in public affairs. We need to carve out of suburbia genuinely public spaces where people can come together.

We have to safeguard individual rights and not stand in the way as people fight to expand them. We have to support local small businesses and local farmers and help one another. No one in Mason should go hungry. 

While working with the Green Party to get ranked-choice voting state-wide, I also would be in favor of promoting its adoption in municipal elections.

Being a legislator would be a paradigm shift for me. I am used to writing on politics, the most I do is organize. Being elected to public office, actually being in power is so much more than saying what ought to be
done, it means doing it. Rousseau in his 'Social Contract' understood this well saying:

>"I shall be asked if I am a prince or a legislator, to write on politics. I answer that I am neither, and that is why I do so. If I were a prince or a legislator, I should not waste time in saying what wants doing; I should do it, or hold my peace."
> ― Jean Jacques Rousseau

My opponents like to say where they stand on national issues to secure their votes. "Oh I'm a pro-life conservative Republican, it doesn't matter that my job has no power in regards to the 
question of abortion, just vote for me." I get it, we like to vote for people we agree with. How many of you would vote for me if I had a 'R' next to my name without knowing who I am or what I stand for? That's part of what's wrong with this country.
If we were on the cusp of banning abortion in the City of Mason maybe I'd make publishing my opinions on the issue
a priority too, but we aren't. That said, I get it. You want to know where I stand on X issue. For that reason
 I have compiled a document detailing my [views](views.pdf) on many of the issues that face us today. This is not comprehensive as there are so, so many issues that face us today. But this is a general summary of where I stand on a variety of issues.
 If a section is lacking or is not satisfactory, please refer to the website of the [US Green Party](https://gp.org) as there's a good chance I agree with my party.
